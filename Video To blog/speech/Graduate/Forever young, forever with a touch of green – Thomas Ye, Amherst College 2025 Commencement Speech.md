# Staying Green, Embracing the Pickle, and Building Bridges

**Class of 2025 Commencement Address**

**Speaker:** Thomas Y., Double Major in Mathematics and Political Science

---

## Introduction: A Graduation Confession

Thank you.

I have to make a confession. I stole something from Amherst and never gave it back. I’ve kept it for four years, and it’s followed me across dorms and semesters. Today, I have to come clean: **I stole a green to-go box.**

Remember these? For those of you who don’t know, Amherst bought these during COVID to encourage eating outside our dining hall. They were a symbol of convenience, avoidance, and—let's be honest—a bit of a stench. Then, suddenly, they were gone. If you ask anyone from the Class of 2026 onwards, they’ll have no idea what we’re talking about.

But we remember.

Some of you might look at these green boxes as an unwelcome reminder of the pandemic. But I see something different. I see a philosophy. Inside this box are three important lessons that I am taking "to go."

---

## Lesson 1: Stay Green

The first lesson is to **stay green**—and I don't just mean eco-friendly green.

Remember how green and naive we were freshman year? In our first month at Amherst, we’d sit next to absolutely anyone in the Valentine dining hall tents. There was no hesitation, no awkwardness—just, *"Hey, mind if I join you?"*

Then, over the course of four years, we collectively forgot how to do that. We started refusing to say hi even when we knew someone. (Yes, I’m looking at you, Casey. Why won’t you say hi to me?) I’ve noticed this past month that a lot of us have suddenly remembered how to be friendly again, simply because the end is near. But why did we wait? I wish we had stayed green and channeled the spirit of freshman year a little more.

I know it’s hard to stay that optimistic. Our class has been through a lot. We survived a global pandemic, witnessed a war in Ukraine, and experienced a major presidential transition. When President Elliot took over from Biddy Martin, everyone was up in arms: *"Ah, another straight white guy. He smiles too much. Why is he lurking at the rave?"*

At first, I was skeptical too. But I’ve come to realize that what initially unsettled us about our friendly neighborhood president is just that **he stayed green**. He embodies the best parts of freshman year: saying hi to everyone, going to every event on campus, and getting himself into plenty of awkward conversations.

I’ve seen this friendly greenness in a few others on campus:

* **Renee**, who brightens everyone’s meal as we enter Val.
* **Professor Contreras** in the math department, who will sit with anyone for breakfast.
* **Damani**, the future CEO of LinkedIn, who will help anyone with their homework at midnight.

What do they all have in common? They kept their greenness.

I’m not telling you to backtrack to freshman year, but as we move forward, let’s not leave behind openness and curiosity. Be intentionally green. When you walk by a stranger, say hello and look at them, not at your phone. When your friends want to make plans, take them out of the group chat and make them happen. Let’s not wait until our 50-year reunion to finally talk to that classmate you’ve always wanted to speak to.

---

## Lesson 2: Embrace the Pickle

The second lesson comes from the **pickle**.

Pickles teach us a lot about how to frame our lives. Fermentation shows us that, in the moment, some things look and smell really bad. But if you give it time, you get something pretty great—like pickles, kombucha, or... kids.

**Embrace the rotten.** Sometimes a horrible, messy process is exactly what makes us better, even if we can only see that in hindsight.

The next time something doesn’t go your way, reframe it:

* When you are suffering through the final weeks of your thesis, you aren’t just in pain—**you are becoming a scholar.**
* When you get a B+ in beginner ballet (like I did), you aren't just failing—**you are pushing your comfort zone.**
* When housing forces you into a Cohan double or a forced triple, find the growth in it.

No matter what you are facing—a tough assignment, an embarrassing moment, or just plain bad luck—those moments are miserable while you're in them. But how are you going to deal with it? Remember the pickle. Reframe it.

My family faced one of these incredibly painful moments last summer with my dad's brain cancer diagnosis. Dad, you’ve beaten the odds, and I am so deeply thankful that you are here with us today. At the time, your sickness was one of the most "rotten" things I had ever experienced. But looking back, that difficult season brought our family closer together than ever. I learned not to hold back my gratitude, my words, or my love. Despite the pain, we all became stronger. We reframed the rotten as growth.

The confidence I have grown into this year didn't come from a string of easy successes; it came from surviving hardship and failure. Many times when I felt down, I was actually on my way up.

In these first post-grad years, you will be tempted to constantly compare your success to others. But fermentation takes time. So will your success. I hope you are lucky enough to not always succeed immediately. I hope you are lucky enough to sometimes fail, and wise enough to wait for the process to finish. The process stinks—sometimes it feels like you're running in circles for no reason—but that is when real progress happens. I did track and field; you can trust me on this.

Be patient. Life is a dream, but sometimes, it’s first a pickle.

## Lesson 3: Build Bridges

The third lesson is the **bridge**.

Bridge is a card game I’ve played every Sunday since coming to Amherst. I’ve played with a lot of seniors—and I don’t just mean my graduating classmates. Usually, when I tell people I play bridge, they say, *"Oh, my grandma plays that."* So how did I get involved with a hobby traditionally reserved for the Gertrudes and Margarets of the world?

It started the way many college activities do: hanging out with friends. But unlike most activities, I got involved because I was bribed during orientation week. A senior told me I could actually get paid to learn a dying card game. So, as an unemployed freshman, I showed up to the North Common Room at 4:00 PM on Sunday. No questions asked.

I hated bridge at first. It’s a incredibly tough game to learn. You sit at a table and play as a partnership with the person across from you. It requires a lot of rules, intense teamwork, and endless frustration. But when that $15 hit my Venmo the next day, I reconsidered.

My friends and I kept showing up week after week. Before long, we grew to love the game itself. We founded the Amherst Bridge Club, traveled to tournaments across the world, and met amazing people. (Full disclosure: we don't pay students to play anymore, so I know it's a tougher sell!)

Even if you never pick up a deck of cards, I hope you will remember the concept of **bridges**. The unexpected gift of this game has been the connections it built for me—bridges that span across generations and geographies.

Bridge is entirely about partnership. At the table, we share strategies, but we also share stories and life lessons. Each table offered a glimpse into a completely different life path. I’ve played with Jeff, a 40-year-old Canadian teacher; Zia, an 80-year-old Pakistani Grandmaster; Bill, a retired biologist; and even Bill Gates. None of those friendships would exist if I hadn't sought out that connection.

Find mentors, and learn who to trust. Not all advice is worth taking—and if you don't believe me on that, you're already doing a great job.

No one does life alone. Lean on our Amherst community. Some of my earliest life direction came from alumni I randomly reached out to—like Mike Reed ('04), AJ Chianese ('05), Ryan Park ('05), and Harran Tong ('23). These alumni responded to emails from a green freshman who knew absolutely nothing about a career in law, and they spent hours talking with me. They built me a bridge.

But remember, bridges go two ways. **What bridge will you build for someone else?**

In just a few minutes, we will all be alumni. Take the time to mentor the interns at your future jobs. Respond to that nervous Amherst freshman who emails you out of the blue. You never know—you might just get a shout-out at their commencement ceremony years from now.

---

## Conclusion: Road Less Traveled

It is finally time for me to give my stolen green box back. But before I do, seniors, take a moment to ask yourselves: **What will you take away from your time at Amherst, and what will you give back to others in the life after?**

It might take us a while to answer that. Amherst isn’t just a singular coordinates of time or place in our lives; it is an eternal community of friends—and a lifelong commitment to explaining to people that, no, we are *different* from UMass Amherst.

When our parents first sent us here, I'm sure they hoped we’d become less naive and finally figure out a concrete life plan. But I actually hope we leave here just as "green" and still a little uncertain about the road ahead.

As the poet for whom our famously ugly Frost Library is named once wrote:

> "Two roads diverged in a wood, and I—
> I took the one less traveled by,
> And that has made all the difference."

I hope you choose the roads not taken. I hope you hold on to your greenness and keep the things that many others would leave behind—because some of those things, like stolen green to-go boxes, are actually worth keeping around.

Before I end, I want to thank President Elliot, the Board of Trustees, the faculty, and all members of the staff. You have truly made Amherst special. Thank you to our families for traveling all this way to support us. While other commencements got Nobel Prize winners or Kermit the Frog, you got me.

Thank you, and congratulations. Class of 2025, it was an absolute pleasure to be young with you. As we depart, let's build bridges, embrace the rotten, and bring our freshman greenness to the world.
